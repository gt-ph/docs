## Full Write-Up

# Problem Statement
## Users
Primary users are Deaf and hard-of-hearing students, particularly those using sign languages (e.g., Singapore Sign Language).
## Use Case
Students need to translate their thoughts from sign into print for many tasks such as assignments, exams, and classroom participation. They encounter this problem daily in educational settings when required to produce written work.
## Pain Point
There is a lack of effective tools and frameworks for cross-modal language mapping between sign and print, compounded by structural differences between the languages.
## Consequence
Deaf students often develop rich, complex ideas in visual languages such as Singapore Sign Language. However, they can face significant barriers when translating those ideas into print.

These challenges can slow literacy development and may misrepresent their cognitive abilities. 
As a result, an artificial gap in academic performance can emerge compared, one that reflects differences in language modality rather than true potential. Over time, this gap can contribute to broader disparities in educational outcomes and opportunities.

How might we better support cross-modal language mapping from sign to print, not only for Deaf students, but for anyone who communicates primarily through sign?
