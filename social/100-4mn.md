## Summary

A companion for caregivers navigating dementia symptoms through diagnosis — so no one falls through the cracks when everything changes at once.

## Full Write-Up

# Problem Statement
## Users
Working-adult caregivers who are the children or children-in-law of dementia patients. In Singapore, dementia cases are projected to surge from 92,000 in 2021 to 152,000 by 2030. Each new diagnosis creates at least one new informal caregiver. Studies show 73% of dementia caregivers in Singapore are the patient's children or children-in-law, dedicating an average of 33 hours per week to caregiving, with most holding full-time jobs.
## Use Case
The problem does not begin at diagnosis. It begins the moment a family member notices something is not right — and has no framework for what to do next. forget-me-not supports caregivers across three distinct and equally under-served phases
  - −6 months: Something feels off, the stage of suspicion without an official label. Amidst uncertainty, a caregiver has to track symptoms, hold difficult conversations, and find the right specialist.
  - Diagnosis (0): Everything changes at this point. There is a flood of immediate action required, with a sense of urgency creeping in (think legal, financial, safety, and medical). Often caregivers don’t know where to begin.
  - +6 months: The ongoing load. Medications, appointments, routines, and caregiver wellbeing, relentless daily operational weight of established caregiving.
## Pain Point
​The absence of any structured support before and immediately around diagnosis. At the pre-diagnosis stage, caregivers are navigating alone with no label, no roadmap, and no one to ask. At diagnosis, information from medical professionals is time-pressured and incomplete. Online resources are generic and unsequenced to the caregiving journey. Caregivers are left piecing together to-dos from memory during the most emotionally turbulent period of their lives. As one caregiver put it: "Every day is a different drama. I don't know what to expect. The information I get feels very generic and not specific enough."
## Consequence
At the pre-diagnosis stage, dementia can go undocumented and undetected for years (WiSE 2023 study found that 51.5% of dementia cases in Singapore remain undiagnosed). At and after diagnosis, the absence of structured guidance means critical steps get routinely missed (e.g. Power of Attorney is not set up before the patient loses legal capacity, home hazards are not addressed until after a fall) and caregiver burden compounds fast (74% of caregivers report feeling overwhelmed, while dementia care costs an average of S$2,483 per month).
# User Research and Market Analysis
## User Research
- Desk research: Reviewed the 2023 Dementia Singapore / Milieu caregiver study, a recent 2026 SMU study with over 10,000 respondents, the WiSE 2023 prevalence study (IMH), the 2024 Singlife dementia caregiving survey, and MOH caregiver burden data.
  - Key insight 1: Pre-diagnosis window is entirely unsupported. No existing tool helps families track early symptoms, prepare for specialist appointments, or cope with the uncertainty of not yet having a diagnosis — despite this being a period of acute stress.
  - Key insight 2: Only 5% of caregivers attended any formal training, yet 38% were unaware subsidised caregiver courses even exist — a massive awareness and access gap that a structured onboarding tool directly addresses.
  - Key insight 3: The recent 2026 SMU study with over 10,000 respondents confirmed that 7 in 10 Singaporeans don't know where to turn when a family member shows signs of dementia.
  - Key insight 4: Caregivers consistently identified the lack of a single phased information source as their biggest friction point. They fall back on reactive Googling as situations arise, piecemealing guidance that should have been front-loaded from the moment something felt wrong.
- User interviews: Spoke to caregivers facing this reality currently. One told us her mind went completely blank at diagnosis — she didn't even know what to Google. Another said the initial things to do were 'aloooot and quite tedious.' A healthcare worker told us caregivers show up before them overwhelmed, fearful, guilty as well.
## De-risking
- Risk: Low digital adoption among older caregivers.
  - Mitigation: Target audience is working-adult children (typically 35–55), who are digitally comfortable. Mobile-first design optimised for use in waiting rooms and on the commute.
- Risk: Information becomes outdated as subsidy schemes change.
  - Mitigation: Partner with Dementia Singapore and AIC for content governance and refresh cycles.
- Risk: Emotional sensitivity — caregivers may disengage if the product feels clinical or overwhelming.
  - Mitigation: Keeping it functional, task-based with an overall warm disposition of a digital companion, anchored in the same voice as the product name.
## Stakeholder Buy-in
Caregiving is a multi-owned space. We had conversations and touchpoints with MOH, MSF and are currently being linked up/directed to have conversations with AIC.
# Solutioning
## Problem-Solution Fit
The journey checklist tells you what to do today. The daily tracker gives your doctors concrete data instead of relying on memory. Everything a caregiver needs, in one place, on the day they need it most.
## Functionality
- For You page is a daily journal tracking both the loved one's symptoms and the caregiver's own wellbeing — data that actually becomes useful at doctor visits.
- Journey tab gives caregivers a step-by-step checklist across medical, legal, financial, housing and social domains — so they always know what to do next.
- Reminders handles medications and appointments.
- Centres surfaces nearby daycare options sorted by distance with subsidy information.
- Community gives caregivers a safe space to ask, share, and not feel alone.
## User Experience
No login required. Empathetic tone and colours. Built to function and feel like a steady hand, not another to-do list.
## User Validation
We put this in front of 11 real users. 81.8% confirmed this addresses a real problem. The top features: caregiving tips and to-do list at 63.6%, medication tracker and care centres directory tied at 54.5%.
Consistent feedback includes: intuitive, linear, easy to navigate, clean. One user called it the 'ten-year series for the caregiving exam' — something they wished had existed when her mum's diagnosis first came.
# Impact & Next Steps
## Success Metrics
- North star is user retention — whether caregivers are coming back and using the app.
- Functionality metric is task completion rate — whether they are actually using the journey checklist and reminders.
- Wellbeing metric is a longitudinal survey on emotional wellbeing improvement — because the point of this product isn't productivity, it's reducing the weight caregivers carry alone.
## Expected Impact
forget-me-not starts with dementia, the infrastructure we've built currently is condition-agnostic. When scaled, priority integration sequence is: first, personalisation because caregiving journeys look different at varying entry points, and secondly, seamless integration to today's system. Thirdly, our users have reviewed today's prototype and influenced us to believe that there is a longer term vision we can pursue, enabling this to be a universal caregiving companion, extending to other chronic care conditions.
