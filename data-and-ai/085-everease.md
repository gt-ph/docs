## Full Write-Up

# Problem Statement
## Users
Elderly
## Use Case
Elderly Singaporeans aged 65 and above who want to maintain an active, independent daily life such as visiting friends, attending community activities, and exploring familiar and new neighbourhoods, but find themselves going out less as their confidence quietly declines. They want reasons to go out, people to go out with, and the reassurance that someone is with them through the journey, not just at the start of it.
## Pain Point
- Declining confidence in navigating public transport and unfamiliar environments, leading to self-imposed restrictions on mobility
- Shrinking social circles and fewer spontaneous social interactions, with no easy way to initiate plans with friends
- Emotional isolation that doesn't always announce itself, felt as flatness, boredom, or a sense that each day is the same as the last
- Existing apps treat them as safety problems to be monitored, rather than people who want to live fuller days
- Family members who care deeply but cannot be present throughout the day, leaving a gap no reminder clock can fill
## Consequence
- Reduced outings compound into reduced social participation and deeper isolation
- Dependence on family for accompaniment to routine activities, increasing caregiver burden
- Loss of autonomy and the sense of being a burden, which further erodes confidence and motivation
- Declining physical and mental wellbeing from reduced activity and social engagement
- Family connections that feel more like check-ins than shared life — moments missed rather than celebrated
# User Research and Market Analysis
## User Research
What user research methods did you adopt to validate that this solution is needed and what were the key insights from real users?
Objective of the User Research: To validate the digital-social isolation gap among HDB-dwelling seniors (65+) and identify interaction design barriers specific to the Singaporean elderly population.
Research Methodology:
We adopted a two-pronged approach combining secondary academic research with primary user evaluation:
1. Peer-reviewed data sources: Duke-NUS Centre for Ageing Research and Education (CARE); ROSA (SMU); Singapore Life Panel — used to establish the prevalence of social isolation, digital exclusion, and technology abandonment patterns among Singaporean seniors.
  Key findings from secondary research:
- The digital-social isolation link is statistically validated. ROSA's 2023 Digital Literacy research brief, drawing on Singapore Life Panel data (n = 6,548 respondents aged 57–76), found that older adults who owned fewer digital devices had significantly higher social isolation scores and lower mental well-being scores, even after controlling for socioeconomic status (Tadai & Tan, ROSA Research Brief, May 2023). This establishes that digital exclusion is not merely an inconvenience — it is a measurable contributor to the isolation that XiaoGuang is designed to address.
- Technology is used for leisure, not function — and fear is the barrier. The same ROSA study found that while 82.26% of seniors used social media and 78.48% watched videos online, only 56.73% used navigation services and only 15.3% used voice assistants. Critically, only 10.49% had used telehealth services. The primary barrier to functional app usage was fear of scams: respondents who lacked confidence in protecting themselves from scams were half as likely to use online banking (30.70%) compared to those who felt confident (60.51%) (Tadai & Tan, ROSA, 2023). This validates XiaoGuang's design decision to build trust through a conversational, companion-like interface rather than a transactional one.
- Seniors can learn technology — but only with human support. ROSA found that older adults were approximately twice as confident learning new digital technologies with assistance from others compared to learning on their own. 93.35% relied on their children, 79.66% on friends, and 67.23% on co-workers for digital learning support (Tadai & Tan, ROSA, 2023). This directly validates XiaoGuang's facilitated onboarding strategy and its positioning as a "24/7 Digital Befriender" that provides the patient, always-available guidance that family members cannot offer throughout the day.
- Government apps are significantly underutilised. 40.65% of seniors surveyed had not successfully used any government mobile application. Even Singpass, the most-used government app, had only been successfully used by 67.3% of respondents, while HealthHub reached only 46.19% and LifeSG only 22.6% (Tadai & Tan, ROSA, 2023). This confirms the "micro-frustration" hypothesis: existing government digital services are not reaching the elderly population they are designed to serve, creating the gap that XiaoGuang fills with voice-first, dialect-speaking interaction.
- Older adults who live alone are not necessarily lonely — but they need active social networks to stay connected. Research by Chua, Ho, and Feng (NUS, SSRC-funded study 2021–2024, n = 1,199 older adults across Taman Jurong and Hougang) found that the number of older adults aged 65+ living alone in Singapore rose from 47,000 in 2016 to 79,000 in 2022, projected to reach 83,000 by 2030 (Ministry of Health, 2023; cited in Chua et al., 2025). However, the researchers distinguish between structural isolation (being physically alone) and perceived isolation (feeling lonely), finding that many solo-living seniors remain deeply connected through dispersed social networks spanning multiple locations — with mean contact distances of 4,163m (Hougang) and 4,377m (Taman Jurong) beyond the household. Crucially, the study found that 66% of named social ties were immediate kin, while only 4.3% were neighbours — meaning seniors' most important relationships are often not geographically proximate. This validates XiaoGuang's "Stay Connected" feature, which brokers social meetups with named friends rather than assuming connection happens automatically through physical proximity.
- Mobility and social network diversity are intertwined. The same NUS study found that the number of different addresses in a respondent's network was positively associated with the number of weak ties (r = .40, p &lt; .001), tie strength diversity (r = .25, p &lt; .001), and racial and educational diversity. The researchers conclude that "transport connectivity, walkability, and affordability must be central to planning age-friendly cities" and that "digital literacy must be seen not as a luxury but a necessity" for older adults to maintain their social ties (Chua et al., 2025). This directly validates XiaoGuang's "Go Out" navigation flow — supporting independent mobility is not just about getting from A to B, but about preserving the social network diversity that protects against isolation.
- The framing matters: ageing in networks, not ageing in place. Chua et al. (2025) propose an "ageing in networks" framework that shifts the focus from where older adults live to with whom they are connected. They argue that dominant paradigms "conflate living alone with being alone, casting solo living in later life as inherently undesirable or isolating," and that older adults are stereotyped as "inactive," "sedentary," "lonely," and having "poor IT skills" (Swift and Steeden, 2020; cited in Chua et al., 2025). XiaoGuang's design philosophy directly aligns with this reframing: it treats elderly users not as safety problems to be monitored but as socially active individuals who need support maintaining their networks across space and time.
1. Primary User Evaluation — STAM Survey (Senior Technology Acceptance Model): We conducted a structured pre-use/post-demo evaluation survey adapted from the validated STAM framework, which extends the standard Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) with constructs specific to older adults: technology anxiety, self-efficacy, facilitating conditions, and social influence. The survey was administered verbally in participants' dialects (Hokkien/Hakka) across a 20-minute guided demo session with 6 elderly participants (ages 65–72, 4 Female / 2 Male).
Key questions to validate:
- What specific "micro-frustrations" lead to elderly abandoning HealthHub, LifeSG, or similar government apps?
- What are some interventions that could help to improve social connection, especially for seniors who are already experiencing loneliness?
- Does a voice-first, dialect-speaking interface meaningfully improve seniors' confidence and willingness to engage with technology?

Qualitative Insights (Verbatim from participants):
- Dialect as trust-builder: Participants reported feeling "more confident in using, and more comfortable" when XiaoGuang spoke in their dialect. The dialect capability shifted the app from feeling like foreign technology to something "meant for me."

## De-risking
What market risks are there and how did you mitigate them?
- Language accuracy risk: Mandarin and dialect speech recognition can be unreliable. Mitigated by using Google Cloud Speech-to-Text with locale-specific tuning and designing the AI system prompt specifically for elderly Singaporean conversational patterns. STAM survey validation: Participants rated dialect interaction positively (C2 qualitative: "feel more confident in using, and more comfortable"), though voice processing latency was flagged as a friction point requiring optimisation.
- Adoption risk: Elderly users may resist new technology. Mitigated by designing a voice-first interface that requires minimal screen interaction — the app behaves more like talking to a helpful friend than using a traditional app. STAM survey validation: Pre-demo technology anxiety was moderate (ANX = 3.50), but self-efficacy jumped from 2.67 to 4.33 after a single 20-minute session, demonstrating that the voice-first design directly addresses the confidence barrier. However, Behavioural Intention (3.83) was conditional on guided onboarding, confirming that design alone is insufficient — facilitated introduction is essential. Secondary research corroboration: ROSA's Singapore Life Panel data (n = 6,548) confirms that older adults are approximately twice as confident learning new technology with human assistance versus on their own, and that 93.35% rely on their children as their primary source of digital learning support (Tadai & Tan, ROSA, 2023) — reinforcing the need for XiaoGuang to serve as the always-available support figure that family members cannot be throughout the day.
- Privacy and trust risk: Elderly users and their families may be uncomfortable with AI monitoring. Mitigated through explicit PDPA-compliant consent flows for both the elderly user and their family members, with transparent data encryption and deletion capabilities. STAM survey validation: Perceived Safety/Privacy scored the highest of all constructs at 4.17/5.0, with no participant raising serious privacy objections. This effectively de-risks the privacy concern for this user segment.
- "Solving the wrong problem" risk: The assumption that elderly isolation is primarily a proximity problem could lead to misguided design. Mitigated by grounding the product thesis in the NUS "Ageing in Networks" research (Chua, Ho & Feng, SSRC 2021–2024), which demonstrates that seniors' most important social ties are dispersed across multiple locations (mean contact distance 4,163–4,377m beyond the household), and that only 4.3% of named social ties are neighbours. This validates XiaoGuang's approach of actively brokering connections with named friends across distance, rather than assuming that co-location in a neighbourhood or activity centre is sufficient for social connection.

Phase 2: Operational (IMDA/AIC & Grassroots)
- Focus: Securing "Site Access" for trials.
- Value: Aligns with "Age Well SG" goals. The "ask" is simple: permission to pilot the prototype at an Active Ageing Centre (AAC) to test real-world impact on senior digital confidence. Additionally, the NUS "Ageing in Networks" research recommends that "community programmes should aim to bring together older adults not just by residential proximity, but by shared interests, values, or life experiences — thus creating opportunities for connection beyond the neighbourhood" and that "traditional service models must evolve to better support dispersed networks" (Chua et al., 2025). XiaoGuang's "Stay Connected" feature directly operationalises this recommendation, giving AACs a tool to extend their impact beyond the physical centre.
# Solutioning
## Problem-Solution Fit
Elderly Singaporeans face a "slow shrinking of their world" where fewer outings lead to confidence declines, thinning social circles, and a growing sense that each day is identical to the last. Crucially, this is framed not as a safety problem but a social vitality problem, and existing apps miss this entirely by offering only reminders and help buttons.
Xiao Guang addresses this at the root. The proactive 2-hourly check-in model flips the paradigm: instead of waiting to be summoned, Xiao Guang initiates contact, reads emotional tone, and acts on signals of loneliness or flatness before they deepen. The Adventure Points system directly attacks the motivation deficit where it turns every outing into something earned and celebrated, with family visibility adding a social layer. And the Stay Connected feature closes the loneliness loop by not just detecting isolation but actively brokering the social fix: identifying a specific friend, drafting the message, and guiding both parties to a meeting point.
Survey validation of problem-solution fit: The STAM survey confirmed that the core pain points are real and that XiaoGuang's approach resonates. Pre-demo, participants reported limited daily companionship (A1 mean: 2.67/5.0) and described AI with apprehension ("don't know how to use"). Post-demo, the transport navigation and friend-finding features were spontaneously cited as the most surprising and valuable capabilities — directly mapping to the "Go Out" and "Stay Connected" flows. The +1.28 attitude shift toward AI after a single session suggests that the companion framing (rather than a monitoring framing) is the right approach for this population.
Secondary research validation of problem-solution fit: The NUS "Ageing in Networks" study (n = 1,199) provides structural evidence for XiaoGuang's design thesis. The finding that seniors' social ties are dispersed across multiple locations — with 66% being immediate kin, 18.5% friends, and only 4.3% neighbours — means that maintaining social connection requires active effort across distance, not just proximity. XiaoGuang's three flows map directly onto this reality: "Talk to Xiao Guang" provides the daily companionship that dispersed kin cannot offer in real-time; "Go Out with Xiao Guang" supports the independent mobility that the researchers identify as essential for maintaining diverse social networks; and "Stay Connected" brokers the cross-distance meetups that keep weak ties alive. Meanwhile, ROSA's finding that seniors who own more digital devices and use more digital applications have significantly higher mental well-being and lower social isolation scores (Tadai & Tan, ROSA, 2023) provides the outcome-level evidence that increasing meaningful digital engagement — which is XiaoGuang's North Star metric — can be expected to improve the very outcomes the product targets.
## Functionality
What are your key user flows/features?
Three core flows, each self-contained but designed to chain into one another:
Talk to Xiao Guang is the universal entry point. The user speaks naturally; Xiao Guang infers intent and routes accordingly — answering queries, initiating social suggestions, or escalating to family if danger is detected. It handles a matrix of signal types (confusion, loneliness, distress, boredom, curiosity) with calibrated responses, and distinguishes sharply between low-risk actions it takes immediately and higher-risk actions it always confirms first.
Go Out with Xiao Guang covers the full journey lifecycle: destination confirmation, mode selection, Maps handoff, mid-journey check-ins, silent family alerts if the user goes quiet, and a celebratory arrival acknowledgement with points awarded. The points system layers on top with streaks, first-visit bonuses, and friend multipliers. This flow was validated as the primary value driver in user testing — participants spontaneously identified the transport function as the most useful feature ("Transport can be easily triggered"), and it was the single most-cited moment of positive surprise during the demo.
Stay Connected handles loneliness detection through both explicit signals ("I'm bored") and ambient signals (flat tone, 48+ hours without social contact). It identifies a named friend, drafts a natural-sounding message, waits for confirmation, and if the friend accepts, chains directly into the navigation flow to find a meetup spot. Participants validated the social connection capability alongside transport: "can easily find friends too."
## User Experience
How did you make the experience seamless/intuitive for the end user?
The experience is built around a single principle: the user never has to navigate a menu or choose a feature. They just talk. Everything else is Xiao Guang's problem to figure out.
Several specific design decisions enforce this. Xiao Guang speaks like a person and uses short sentences, direct answers, never more than two choices at once. The UI uses large buttons, large text, high contrast, and caps visible home-screen actions at five. No menus go deeper than two levels. Every response ends with three simple recovery options: repeat slower, explain simpler, ask something else, giving the user a dignified exit from any moment of confusion without having to admit they're lost.
For the family, the adventure log reframes surveillance into something warm such as "Mum went to East Coast Park with Lucy yesterday, 80 points" is a dinner conversation starter, not a monitoring report. The whole product is designed so the elderly user feels independent and the family feels at ease, without either feeling like the other is watching.
User testing validation of UX decisions:
The STAM survey validated several core UX decisions and surfaced specific improvements:
- Voice-first interaction validated: Perceived Ease of Use scored 3.83/5.0 on first use, and self-efficacy jumped +1.67 pre-to-post — confirming that the "just talk" paradigm works for this demographic.
- Button/menu minimalism validated: One participant explicitly noted "still quite comfortable, but if too many buttons then will get confused" — confirming the five-action home screen cap is the right constraint.
- Dialect interaction validated: Participants felt "more confident in using, and more comfortable" when XiaoGuang spoke their dialect, confirming this as a core differentiator rather than a nice-to-have.
Improvements identified from testing:
- Voice processing latency: "The voice takes a bit long to process" was flagged — response time optimisation is needed to maintain the conversational flow that makes the app feel like talking to a person rather than waiting for a machine.
- Guided onboarding is essential: The conditional nature of adoption responses ("yes, but with teaching and guidance") confirms that a structured first-use walkthrough — ideally facilitated by a family member or community volunteer — must be designed as a core product feature, not an optional tutorial.
## User Validation
What feedback did users give about the product? What worked well and what required improvement?
Validation Methodology:
We conducted a STAM (Senior Technology Acceptance Model) evaluation survey with 6 elderly participants (ages 65–72, 4 Female / 2 Male, Hokkien and Hakka speakers). The survey comprised a pre-use baseline questionnaire administered verbally in participants' dialects, a 20-minute guided demo session where the facilitator stayed present but did not lead, and an immediate post-demo questionnaire. Both quantitative Likert scores (1–5) and verbatim qualitative responses were captured.
What Worked Well:
1. Self-efficacy showed the largest improvement of any construct (+1.67): Before the demo, seniors rated their confidence to use XiaoGuang independently at 2.67/5.0 (below neutral). After just 20 minutes, this jumped to 4.33/5.0 (above "Agree"). This is the headline finding — the voice-first, dialect-speaking design directly addresses the confidence deficit that underpins the entire problem statement. Participants who entered the session saying "quite worried, overwhelmed with many new functions" left saying they could "most likely" use it on their own.
1. Attitude toward AI shifted meaningfully from cautious to positive (+1.28): Pre-demo, the word "AI" triggered apprehension — "don't know how to use, children no time to teach." Post-demo, the same participants described the experience as "quite easy to use" and noted their feelings had changed "a little" for the better. This demonstrates that hands-on experience with a well-designed interface is the most effective antidote to technology anxiety among elderly users.
1. Technology anxiety reduced meaningfully after a single session (+1.00): Pre-demo, seniors scored 3.00/5.0 on reversed anxiety (moderate anxiety present). Post-demo, this improved to 4.00/5.0 (low anxiety). Participants who entered the session "quite worried, overwhelmed with many new functions" reported feeling only "a little" nervous post-demo, and four of six participants showed clear anxiety reduction. This confirms that the voice-first design and dialect interaction actively reduce — rather than merely accommodate — technology anxiety.
1. Privacy and safety trust scored highest of all constructs (4.17/5.0): No participant raised serious privacy objections. This effectively de-risks the privacy concern and validates the PDPA-compliant consent design and the conversational (rather than surveillance-oriented) product framing.
1. Transport navigation was spontaneously identified as the most valuable feature: Without prompting, participants cited the transport function as the most useful and most surprising element — "Transport can be easily triggered, and can easily find friends too." This validates the "Go Out with Xiao Guang" journey flow as the product's primary value driver and confirms that the problem-solution fit is strongest around independent mobility.
1. Perceived Ease of Use scored 3.83/5.0 on first use: For a population that self-reported moderate technology anxiety, achieving a near-4.0 ease-of-use score on the very first interaction validates the voice-first, minimal-GUI design philosophy.
What Required Improvement:
1. Perceived Usefulness dipped slightly post-demo (−0.44): Pre-demo expectations (3.83) were higher than post-demo perceived usefulness (3.39). This suggests the 20-minute demo protocol did not fully showcase the app's breadth of capability — particularly the transport and social features that participants later cited as most valuable. Future testing should restructure the demo to lead with the transport navigation flow and include a social connection scenario.
1. Behavioural Intention is positive but conditional (3.83/5.0): This falls just short of the 4.0 "strong intent" threshold. Critically, both G1 (want to use again) and G2 (would recommend) qualitative responses included the same caveat: "yes, but with teaching and guidance." This confirms that adoption depends on facilitated onboarding — the product cannot rely on self-serve discovery. A structured first-use walkthrough, ideally facilitated by a family member, community volunteer, or Silver Generation Ambassador, must be designed as a core part of the rollout strategy.
1. The oldest participant (P6, age 72) scored consistently lowest across all constructs: Attitude (1.75), Self-Efficacy (2.00), and Behavioural Intention (2.50) were all well below the group mean. This indicates that the oldest and least digitally-exposed segment faces compounding barriers that the current design does not fully overcome. A dedicated "first-time" mode with even simpler interaction patterns, or a longer facilitated onboarding period, may be needed for this sub-segment.
1. Concrete UI improvement requested — colour-coded emergency contacts: "Allow emergency contacts to be of different colour, since words may be hard to see, colour can help elderly define which are the contacts of importance." This is a specific, low-effort, high-impact design change that should be prioritised in the next sprint.
Planned Next-Phase Validation:
1. High-Fidelity Scenario Testing (Voice Accuracy): We will conduct structured usability tests focusing on the Voice User Interface (VUI) with minimal GUI interaction across 2–3 critical scenarios: Travel assistance and Social Check-ins.
  - Metric: Achieve a >80% Task Completion Rate where seniors successfully navigate the request from trigger-word to resolution without verbal or physical intervention from facilitators.
  - Focus: Evaluation of "Linguistic Resilience" — the app's ability to parse code-switching between Mandarin and common dialects (Hokkien/Cantonese). The current survey covered Hokkien and Hakka speakers only; Cantonese, Teochew, and Malay must be included in subsequent rounds.
1. Ethnographic "Kopi-Chat" Sessions: Instead of a sterile lab environment, qualitative feedback will be gathered through facilitated focus group discussions at Active Ageing Centres (AACs).
  - Stakeholders: 4–6 elderly residents per session, joined by a Community Leader or Silver Generation Ambassador (SGA) to act as a cultural bridge.
  - Objective: To capture nuanced feedback on the "Emotional UX" — moving beyond "does it work?" to "does it feel like a trusted companion?" This is particularly important given the STAM finding that attitude toward AI improved significantly with hands-on experience — the Kopi-Chat format will test whether this improvement sustains over repeated interactions in a naturalistic setting.

## Expected Impact
What outcomes do you expect this to be able to deliver in the short/medium/long term if your product is launched to users?
- Short-term (0–3 months): Elderly users gain a reliable Mandarin-speaking companion for daily conversation and questions, reducing immediate feelings of loneliness. Early adopters successfully complete independent bus journeys to routine destinations (community market) without family accompaniment. Elderly connects with their friends by using the application. The STAM survey provides early evidence for this: self-efficacy jumped from 2.67 to 4.33 after a single 20-minute session, and the transport function was spontaneously identified as the most useful feature. The immediate priority is to refine the onboarding experience based on the finding that adoption is conditional on "teaching and guidance."
- Medium-term (3–12 months): Measurable increase in elderly users' confidence to travel independently and engage with digital services. Family members report reduced anxiety through proactive WhatsApp notifications. Partnership with Senior Activity Centres enables scaled distribution. The survey's +1.28 attitude shift toward AI and 4.17/5.0 privacy trust score provide a strong foundation for family buy-in and institutional partnership conversations. The colour-coded emergency contacts and text size improvements identified in testing will be implemented to improve accessibility for the oldest users (72+) who showed the lowest scores. At this stage, XiaoGuang's impact can be benchmarked against ROSA's baseline finding that seniors with greater digital engagement have significantly higher mental well-being and lower social isolation scores (Tadai & Tan, ROSA, 2023), providing an evidence-based framework for measuring whether increased voice interactions translate into improved well-being outcomes.
- Long-term (1–3 years): Xiao Guang becomes a trusted digital companion platform for elderly Singaporeans, potentially expanding to include telemedicine appointment booking, government service navigation, and community event discovery. The voice-first interaction model serves as a reference for inclusive digital service design across Smart Nation initiatives, ensuring no elderly Singaporean is left behind in the digital transition. The STAM framework used in this evaluation provides a repeatable measurement methodology that can track construct scores longitudinally as the product scales — enabling evidence-based iteration at every stage. Long-term, XiaoGuang operationalises the "ageing in networks" paradigm proposed by Chua, Ho & Feng (2025): rather than confining elderly support to place-based services, it enables seniors to maintain and activate dispersed social networks through voice-initiated connection and assisted mobility — supporting the researchers' recommendation that "traditional service models must evolve to better support dispersed networks, for instance, through telehealth, interest-based virtual communities, or cross-neighbourhood events."
