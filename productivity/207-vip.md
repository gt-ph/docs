## Full Write-Up

# Problem Statement
## Users
Approximately 200 Protocol officers and MFA officers both in HQ and overseas, plus potentially another 1,000 officers covering international relations in other agencies. These officers collectively manage over 30-40 Head of State and Head of Government visits per year and another 200 Ministerial-level visits across government annually.
Secondary Users:
Visit delegates and leadership who need real-time access to updated programmes, seating arrangements, and itinerary changes without relying on manual coordination chains.
## Use Case
Officers are responsible for planning and executing every element of incoming and outgoing high-level visits: drafting the Order of Proceedings, preparing seating plans, coordinating motorcade routes, and distributing accurate programmes to all parties. This work spans 1-3 months of preparation and intensifies in the final 24-48 hours before a visit.
The critical challenge: Changes to routes, guest lists, timings, and venues occur at every stage—including during the visit itself. When a change happens, officers must manually update multiple documents, notify relevant parties (security, transport, venues, restaurants, hotels) depending on which parts of the itinerary are affected, resend programmes, and hope every party receives and reads the update in time.
## Pain Point
The current coordination process is highly manual and fragmented. A single itinerary change can trigger cascading updates across multiple operational documents and stakeholders. Officers must manually determine who is affected, regenerate plans individually, and communicate updates through separate channels. Manual processes for generating and disseminating plan changes can be time-consuming and error-prone.
## Consequence
Without automation, continued inefficiencies waste officer time, and manual errors could lead to confusion, meeting delays, or coordination failures during high-stakes diplomatic visits.
Protocol failures create diplomatic risk—outdated seating plans, missed motorcade timings, and incorrect programmes can reflect poorly on the hosting government. Protocol officers experience significant stress and work outside of office hours on every major visit. The cumulative operational burden is severe.
# User Research and Market Analysis
## User Research
We conducted structured user research with protocol officers and advance team members:
- Service Design Thinking Workshop with protocol officers to generate the service blueprint and understand the user journey, prioritising key pain points and the problem statement.
- Contextual interviews with 8 protocol officers, uncovering detailed pain points around change coordination, WhatsApp usage, and programme distribution.
- Prototype usability testing with officers using the MVP for seating plan generation alone.
Key insights from user research:
- 100% of interviewed officers reported using WhatsApp as the primary coordination tool for visit changes.
- All officers reported regenerating plans at least once per visit cycle due to last-minute changes.
- Officers manually maintain multiple versions of operational documents.
- Untimely information delivery leads to miscommunications and misunderstandings.
- The seating plan generator was identified as the highest-value feature. It was reported that officers currently spend 30 minutes manually drafting seating plans and with the seating plan generator feature, the time spent was reduced to under 60 seconds.
## De-risking
What market risks are there and how did you mitigate them?
Our product assumes operational coordination is painful enough that officers are willing to adopt a dedicated digital platform.
We validated this through user interviews and workflow mapping sessions, where officers consistently highlighted manual coordination and change management as key operational pain points.
To reduce adoption risk, Visit Planner was designed around existing operational workflows and terminology such as OOPs, seating plans, and stakeholder lists. The system focuses on augmenting current workflows instead of requiring officers to redesign their processes entirely.
We first developed just the seating plan generator to validate genuine user demand beyond the protocol team and to build user confidence. We generated 147 seating plans in 4 months, with a high user satisfaction score of 8.5/10 on our recommendation metric.
## Stakeholder Buy-in
We reached out to MFA officers involved in protocol, and operational planning teams. Initial discussions indicated strong interest and support for the initiative.
The use case also demonstrated potential applicability across WOG. Agencies like MINDEF and AGC had previously expressed interest in a similar application.
# Solutioning
## Problem-Solution Fit
How does your product specifically address the pain points identified?
Visit Planner centralises visit planning and operational coordination into a single platform. Officers can make a single update within the system, after which Visit Planner automatically identifies affected plans and stakeholders, and regenerates operational documents.
Functionality — Key Features
01 Summary Programme | Build, manage, and publish the full visit programme.
02 Motorcade Planner | Route builder with vehicle and personnel assignments.
03 Seating Plan Generator | Intelligent seating using protocol precedence rules.
04 AI Companion | AI Assistant to inform delegates of their involvement using up-to-date information.
05 Change Handling | Last-minute changes propagate instantly. Every action is logged, timestamped, and attributable for post-visit audit.
## Functionality
What are your key user flows/features?
1. Officer creates or updates a visit programme.
1. A change is made to the programme (e.g. venue or schedule adjustment).
1. Visit Planner automatically identifies impacted plans.
1. Operational documents such as seating plans, and motorcade plans are regenerated.
1. Relevant stakeholders receive the updated plans upon request.
## User Experience
How did you make the experience seamless/intuitive for the end user?
Visit Planner is designed for on-the-ground use under time pressure. Key UX decisions include:
- Familiar terminology (OOPs, seating plans, delegation lists) to minimise learning curve.
- High visibility of information such as delegates and plans.
- Clear visibility into operational impacts.
## User Validation
What feedback did users give about the product?
Users expressed that the application is on the right track, noting that it provides a useful snapshot of what a visit entails and is easy to navigate. It will also serve as a good repository of past visits for reference. Further development and testing will be required to cover different scenarios and visit types.
# Impact & Next Steps
## Success Metrics
What is your "North Star" metric?
Time saved per officer per month, given that our problem-solution fit is to address the core pain point of manual coordination inefficiencies.
## Expected Impact
What outcomes do you expect this to be able to deliver in the short/medium/long term if your product is launched to users?
Short Term (6-12 months)
- Efficiency gains: 50% reduction in time spent on seating plans generation and motorcade management
- Adoption: Support both high-level incoming and outgoing visits involving HOS or HOG
Medium Term (1-2 years)
- Efficiency gains: Deliver a 30% productivity improvement across visit management workflows
- Scale adoption: Deploy to all MFA officers to include Ministerial and non-Ministerial visits
Long Term (>2 years)
- Strategic capability: Enhanced operational responsiveness during high-stakes bilateral and multilateral visits
- Ecosystem adoption: Extend use to other government agencies managing international engagements
